# Legal & IP — Read This Before You Sell Anything

*General information for an Australian small business, not legal advice. The
image-rights issue here is real and specific — if you're unsure, get an hour of
advice from an IP lawyer (Sprintlaw, LawPath, or an IP specialist) before you
scale.*

---

## The core problem: you're selling images of other people's brands

Petrol Post's product is **prints and photos of specific, famous cars** —
Ferrari, Porsche, Lamborghini, Nissan Skyline, Holden, Ford, etc. Two separate
bodies of law apply, and both can bite:

### 1. Copyright (in the photographs and artwork)
- A **photograph is owned by whoever took it** (or their employer/commissioner).
- You **cannot** pull a car photo off Google, a forum, or a manufacturer's
  press site and print/sell it. That's copyright infringement, even if you
  "found it online."
- The same applies to existing artwork, blueprints and posters.

### 2. Trademark & trade dress (in the brands)
- Car **names, logos and badges** (the Ferrari shield, the Porsche crest, "GT-R",
  "Mustang") are **registered trademarks**. Using them to sell merchandise can
  infringe, and marques like Ferrari are famously aggressive about enforcement.
- Even the distinctive **shape/design** of some cars can be protected.

Building a subscription business on unlicensed brand imagery is the single most
likely way this venture gets a cease-and-desist or worse. **Solve this first.**

## Three legitimate ways to get your imagery

### Option 1 — Commission original artwork (recommended)
- Hire illustrators to create **original interpretations** — stylised art,
  blueprints, cutaways — and buy **full commercial rights / assignment** so you
  own the output.
- Original art of a car's *silhouette or form* for editorial/artistic purposes
  sits on much safer ground than copying a photo or reproducing a logo.
- **Avoid reproducing the badge/logo/wordmark.** Depict the car, not the brand's
  trademarks. Don't imply the marque endorses you.

### Option 2 — Licence photography properly
- Buy licences from stock libraries (Getty/iStock, Shutterstock, Adobe Stock)
  and **read the licence terms** — you specifically need rights that cover
  **print and physical merchandise / resale**, not just "editorial" or
  "digital only" use.
- Or licence directly from automotive photographers (many will licence prints
  for a fee or revenue share).

### Option 3 — Shoot your own photography
- Photograph cars at shows, meets, and via owners/clubs, with a simple
  **model/property release** or written permission. You own the photo; be
  mindful you still shouldn't foreground protected logos as the selling point.
- This also makes the product *more* Australian and authentic (local cars,
  local events) — a genuine differentiator vs the UK original.

### What to avoid
- ❌ Copying photos or art you found online.
- ❌ Reproducing manufacturer logos/badges/wordmarks on merch.
- ❌ Implying official endorsement or partnership you don't have.
- ❌ Using AI to clone a specific brand's protected trade dress.

> **Safest editorial framing:** original artwork + your own/licensed photography
> + "blueprint" style + storytelling. You're selling *car culture and art*, and
> you're careful not to trade off any marque's trademarks. Consider a small
> disclaimer: *"An independent publication. Not affiliated with or endorsed by
> any vehicle manufacturer."*

## Consumer law — subscriptions & auto-renewal (Australian Consumer Law)

Subscriptions are squarely in the ACCC's sights. To comply with the **Australian
Consumer Law (ACL)**:

- **Be upfront:** clearly state the price, that it **auto-renews**, and the
  billing frequency **before** the customer pays.
- **No pre-ticked boxes:** the customer must actively opt in to recurring billing.
- **Easy cancellation:** make cancelling as easy as signing up (self-serve in the
  account, or one email). Don't trap people.
- **No misleading claims:** every claim in your ads and on the site must be true
  ("save $40," "limited to 100," testimonials must be genuine).
- **Consumer guarantees:** goods must be of acceptable quality and match their
  description; handle faulty/lost drops fairly (reship or refund).
- ACCC business guidance: <https://www.accc.gov.au/business>

## Policies you must publish on the site

| Policy | Why |
|--------|-----|
| **Terms of Service** | The contract for your subscription; billing, delivery, cancellation. |
| **Subscription & Cancellation terms** | ACL requires clear auto-renewal + cancel info. |
| **Privacy Policy** | Required once you collect names/emails/addresses; Shopify & Stripe expect it. |
| **Shipping Policy** | Ship dates (1st of month), regions, timing, lost-mail handling. |
| **Refund / Returns Policy** | Your approach to faulty/lost/damaged drops and change-of-mind. |

Shopify provides editable templates. For ~$200–500, services like **Sprintlaw**
or **LawPath** will tailor a proper AU set — worth it once you have real revenue.

## Protecting *your* brand

- **Register your business name** with ASIC (done in the checklist).
- **Search IP Australia** before committing to "Revhead Post" (or any name) to
  avoid infringing an existing trademark:
  <https://search.ipaustralia.gov.au/trademarks/search/quick>
- **Register your own trademark** once you're committed (protects the brand you
  build): TM Headstart from ~$250/class. <https://www.ipaustralia.gov.au>
- Note: "Petrol Post" is an existing UK brand — **do not copy their name, logo,
  or exact copy.** Build your own Australian identity. Adapting the *business
  model* is fine (models aren't protectable); copying their *brand and
  creative* is not.

## Privacy & data

- You'll hold names, emails and postal addresses — treat them carefully.
- Small businesses under $3M turnover may be exempt from parts of the Privacy
  Act, but follow best practice: a clear privacy policy, secure storage (Shopify
  handles most of this), and don't sell customer data.
- OAIC guidance: <https://www.oaic.gov.au>

---

## Tax obligations (quick reference — see an accountant)

- **Income tax:** as a sole trader you report business profit on your personal
  return; keep every receipt and record all income.
- **GST:** register once turnover hits **$75,000/yr**; then add 10% GST and lodge
  BAS. <https://www.ato.gov.au>
- **Records:** keep financial records for **5 years**.
- **Deductions:** printing, postage, packaging, Shopify, ads, software, home
  office, phone/internet portion, and more are typically deductible.

---

## One-page "don't get sued" summary

1. **Own or licence every image.** Commission art, licence photos, or shoot your
   own. Never lift images off the web.
2. **Don't put manufacturer logos/badges on merch** or imply endorsement.
3. **Make auto-renewal clear and cancellation easy** (ACL).
4. **Publish honest, complete policies** (ToS, privacy, shipping, refunds).
5. **Use your own brand name** — check IP Australia first, then register it.
6. **Keep clean records** and register for GST at $75k.
